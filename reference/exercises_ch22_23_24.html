Chapter 22 — Comparison of Two Variances

Tests covered: the F-test for \(\sigma_1^2/\sigma_2^2\) (one- and two-sided variants) [slide 498], the reciprocal-tail identity \(f_{1-\alpha,\nu_1,\nu_2} = 1/f_{\alpha,\nu_2,\nu_1}\) [slide 496], confidence intervals for the variance ratio, OC curves with abscissa \(\lambda = \sigma_1/\sigma_2\) [slide 501], and the pre-test caveat (when the F-test feeds a downstream means test, control \(\beta\), not \(\alpha\)) [slide 499]. The trap pattern: writing the wrong tail of the F-distribution (mismatched degrees-of-freedom subscripts) or pre-testing for variance equality and then deciding which two-sample T to use on the same data.

Ch 22 — Q1
Easy F-test, two-sided
Original. Direct application of slide 498.
Two independent random samples are drawn from normal populations. Sample 1: \(n_1 = 10\), \(s_1^2 = 8.4\). Sample 2: \(n_2 = 12\), \(s_2^2 = 3.1\). At \(\alpha = 0.05\), test \(H_0:\sigma_1^2 = \sigma_2^2\) vs.\ \(H_1:\sigma_1^2\neq\sigma_2^2\). State the decision and the (approximate) two-sided P-value.
Reveal solution

Assumptions. Independent random samples drawn from normal populations — essential for the F-test [slide 499].

Hypotheses. \(H_0:\sigma_1=\sigma_2\) vs.\ \(H_1:\sigma_1\neq\sigma_2\) (two-sided).

Statistic. Under \(H_0\), \(\;F = S_1^2/S_2^2 \sim F_{n_1-1,\,n_2-1} = F_{9,11}\) [slide 498].

Computation. \(F = 8.4/3.1 = 2.71\).

Rejection region (course convention, slide 498). Reject if \(S_1^2/S_2^2 > f_{\alpha/2,\,n_1-1,\,n_2-1} = f_{0.025,\,9,\,11} \approx 3.59\) or \(S_2^2/S_1^2 > f_{\alpha/2,\,n_2-1,\,n_1-1} = f_{0.025,\,11,\,9} \approx 3.91\).

Decision. Here \(2.71 < 3.59\) and \(S_2^2/S_1^2 = 0.369 < 3.91\), so do not reject \(H_0\).

P-value. One-sided upper-tail \(P[F_{9,11}\ge 2.71] \approx 0.063\); doubled for two-sided \(\approx 0.13\).

Grading rubric — minimum points to mention
  1. State normality assumption and that the F-test requires it — 1 pt
  2. Identify \(F = S_1^2/S_2^2 \sim F_{n_1-1,n_2-1}\) under \(H_0\) — 2 pts
  3. Compute \(F = 2.71\) (right ratio) — 1 pt
  4. Quote both critical values \(f_{0.025,9,11}\) and \(f_{0.025,11,9}\) (course's two-sided convention) — 2 pts
  5. Decision (do not reject) and a contextual sentence — 2 pts
  6. P-value \(\approx 0.13\) — 1 pt
Total: 9 pts
Trail of thought — how to attack this question

Slide 498 writes the two-sided rejection region as two right-tail conditions, one with the larger sample variance on top and one with the other ordering. That is just the reciprocal-tail trick (slide 496) in disguise: rather than look up a left-tail point \(f_{1-\alpha/2,\,9,\,11}\), flip the ratio and check the right tail with the degrees of freedom swapped. Common slip: writing \(f_{0.025,\,11,\,9}\) when the larger \(s^2\) is in sample 1 — check which tail is the relevant one before reaching for the table.

Ch 22 — Q2
Easy F-distribution reciprocal identity
Original. Practises slide 496.
Standard tables list the right-tail critical value \(f_{0.05,\,8,\,5} = 4.82\). Use the reciprocal identity to find the left-tail critical value \(f_{0.95,\,5,\,8}\), and explain its meaning.
Reveal solution

Slide 496 gives \(\;f_{1-\alpha,\,\nu_1,\,\nu_2} = 1/f_{\alpha,\,\nu_2,\,\nu_1}\). Apply with \(\alpha=0.05\), \(\nu_1=5\), \(\nu_2=8\): \[ f_{0.95,\,5,\,8} = \frac{1}{f_{0.05,\,8,\,5}} = \frac{1}{4.82} \approx 0.207. \]

Meaning. \(P[F_{5,8} > 0.207] = 0.95\), or equivalently, \(P[F_{5,8} < 0.207] = 0.05\). This is the cut-off below which a left-tailed F-test of \(H_0:\sigma_1\ge\sigma_2\) (using \(F = S_1^2/S_2^2\)) would reject at \(\alpha=0.05\).

Grading rubric — minimum points to mention
  1. Quote slide-496 identity \(f_{1-\alpha,\nu_1,\nu_2} = 1/f_{\alpha,\nu_2,\nu_1}\) — 2 pts
  2. Note that both the order of the d.f.\ and the tail flip — 1 pt
  3. Compute \(0.207\) — 1 pt
  4. Interpret as a left-tail cut-off — 1 pt
Total: 5 pts
Trail of thought — how to attack this question

Two things flip simultaneously: (i) the tail (\(\alpha\) becomes \(1-\alpha\)), and (ii) the order of the degrees of freedom (\((\nu_1,\nu_2)\) becomes \((\nu_2,\nu_1)\)). Forgetting the swap of d.f.\ is the most common error and gives a wildly wrong answer because the F-distribution is not symmetric. Sanity check: a left-tail point should be small (between 0 and 1) and a right-tail point at the same \(\alpha\) on the swapped d.f.\ should be large (greater than 1). Here \(0.207 < 1 < 4.82\) — consistent.

Ch 22 — Q3
Easy F-test, one-sided
Original; structure echoes ve401_summer21_ex08.pdf.
A process engineer claims that line A has greater variability than line B. Independent samples give \(n_1 = 15\), \(s_1^2 = 5.6\) (line A) and \(n_2 = 15\), \(s_2^2 = 2.1\) (line B). At \(\alpha = 0.05\), test \(H_0:\sigma_1\le\sigma_2\) vs.\ \(H_1:\sigma_1>\sigma_2\). State the decision and the P-value.
Reveal solution

Statistic. Under the boundary of \(H_0\), \(\;F = S_1^2/S_2^2 \sim F_{14,14}\).

Computation. \(F = 5.6/2.1 = 2.667\).

Rejection region (slide 498, one-sided upper). Reject if \(F > f_{0.05,\,14,\,14} \approx 2.484\). Since \(2.667 > 2.484\), reject \(H_0\).

P-value. \(P[F_{14,14}\ge 2.667] \approx 0.038\). There is evidence at the 5% level that line A is more variable than line B.

Grading rubric — minimum points to mention
  1. Set up one-sided \(H_0/H_1\) and use \(F=S_1^2/S_2^2\) (claim of bigger variance goes in \(H_1\)) — 1 pt
  2. Identify \(F_{14,14}\) as the null distribution — 1 pt
  3. Compute \(F = 2.67\) — 1 pt
  4. Quote the one-sided critical value \(f_{0.05,14,14}=2.48\) (not \(f_{0.025,\ldots}\)) — 1 pt
  5. Correct decision (reject) — 1 pt
  6. P-value \(\approx 0.038\) and contextual interpretation — 2 pts
Total: 7 pts
Trail of thought — how to attack this question

For a one-sided alternative \(\sigma_1 > \sigma_2\), the critical value is \(f_{\alpha,\,\nu_1,\,\nu_2}\), not \(f_{\alpha/2}\). The most common error is to halve \(\alpha\) out of habit from two-sided tests — that pulls the cut-off up to \(f_{0.025,14,14}\approx 2.98\) and would (incorrectly) flip the verdict. Also note: putting line A in the numerator was deliberate. If the question said "test whether B is more variable than A", you would either compute \(S_2^2/S_1^2 = 0.375\) and compare to \(f_{0.05,14,14}\) (clearly fail to reject), or equivalently compare the original \(F=2.67\) to a left-tail point.

Ch 22 — Q4
Medium F-test as pre-test, β control
Original; uses the slide-499 caveat directly.
An engineer plans to use a Student (pooled) two-sample T-test on two samples with \(n_1=n_2=10\) and wants to first justify the equal-variance assumption with an F-test. The samples give \(s_1^2 = 7.8\) and \(s_2^2 = 4.6\).
  1. Why should the F-test in this setting be conducted at a larger significance level (e.g.\ \(\alpha=0.10\) or \(0.20\)) rather than the usual 5%?
  2. Carry out the F-test at \(\alpha = 0.10\). Does the data justify proceeding with the pooled T-test?
Reveal solution

(i) Per slide 499: when the F-test is being used as a screening step before a downstream comparison-of-means test, "passing" the F-test (i.e.\ failing to reject equality of variances) is what we want. In that role the relevant error rate is \(\beta\) (Type II), not \(\alpha\). Because the F-test is not very powerful, raising \(\alpha\) shrinks \(\beta\) and improves the chance that a real inequality of variances will be caught before the means test runs. (Note: this is a textbook caveat — current best practice, slide 527, is to skip the pre-test entirely and use Welch's T-test always.)

(ii) \(F = 7.8/4.6 = 1.696\). Two-sided 10% rejection region: \(F > f_{0.05,\,9,\,9} \approx 3.18\) or \(F < 1/3.18 \approx 0.314\). \(1.696\) is comfortably inside the acceptance region; do not reject equality of variances. Subject to the slide-527 caveat above, the engineer may proceed with the pooled T-test.

Grading rubric — minimum points to mention
  1. Explain that the controlled error in this role is \(\beta\), not \(\alpha\) — 2 pts
  2. Note the F-test is "not very powerful" — raising \(\alpha\) reduces \(\beta\) — 1 pt
  3. Mention slide-527 caveat (Welch's preferred regardless) — 1 pt
  4. Compute \(F = 1.70\) and the 10% two-sided cut-offs — 2 pts
  5. Decision: do not reject equality — 1 pt
Total: 7 pts
Trail of thought — how to attack this question

The conceptual move is to flip the usual mindset: in most tests, we set \(\alpha\) and let \(\beta\) fall where it may. Here, because we want to fail to reject (so we can use the pooled T), the cost of a Type II error (using pooled when variances are actually unequal) is high, while the cost of a Type I error (switching to Welch unnecessarily) is low. So spend power, raise \(\alpha\). The course's modern position (slide 527) is even more conservative: skip the F-test and use Welch's T-test always — mention that as the principled alternative.

Ch 22 — Q6
Medium CI for σ₁²/σ₂²
Original. Derived from slide 497 plus the reciprocal identity.
Construct a 95% confidence interval for \(\sigma_1^2/\sigma_2^2\) given \(n_1 = 10\), \(s_1^2 = 4.0\), \(n_2 = 8\), \(s_2^2 = 9.0\). Use this interval to decide \(H_0:\sigma_1=\sigma_2\) vs.\ \(H_1:\sigma_1\neq\sigma_2\) at the two-sided 5% level.
Reveal solution

Starting from \(F = (S_1^2/\sigma_1^2)/(S_2^2/\sigma_2^2) \sim F_{9,7}\), invert the probability statement \(f_{0.975,9,7} \le F \le f_{0.025,9,7}\): \[ \frac{S_1^2/S_2^2}{f_{0.025,\,9,\,7}} \;\le\; \frac{\sigma_1^2}{\sigma_2^2}\;\le\; \frac{S_1^2/S_2^2}{f_{0.975,\,9,\,7}} = \frac{S_1^2}{S_2^2}\cdot f_{0.025,\,7,\,9}. \] With \(S_1^2/S_2^2 = 4/9 = 0.444\), \(f_{0.025,9,7} \approx 4.82\), \(f_{0.025,7,9} \approx 4.20\): \[ \frac{0.444}{4.82} \le \frac{\sigma_1^2}{\sigma_2^2}\le 0.444\cdot 4.20 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; \big[0.092,\,1.866\big]. \]

Test by duality. The CI contains \(1\), so we do not reject \(H_0:\sigma_1=\sigma_2\) at the two-sided 5% level. (Equivalently: \(F = 0.444 < f_{0.025,9,7}\) and \(1/F = 2.25 < f_{0.025,7,9}\), consistent with non-rejection.)

Grading rubric — minimum points to mention
  1. Pivot through \((S_1^2/\sigma_1^2)/(S_2^2/\sigma_2^2)\sim F_{n_1-1,n_2-1}\) — 2 pts
  2. Use the reciprocal identity for the lower bound — 1 pt
  3. Quote both critical values \(f_{0.025,9,7}\) and \(f_{0.025,7,9}\) — 1 pt
  4. Compute CI \([0.092,\,1.866]\) — 2 pts
  5. Use the duality: \(1\) in CI \(\Leftrightarrow\) fail to reject — 1 pt
Total: 7 pts
Trail of thought — how to attack this question

The CI for a variance ratio is not symmetric around the point estimate — it is multiplicative, of the form \([\hat r/c_1,\,\hat r\cdot c_2]\). Note the asymmetry of the d.f.\ subscripts: the lower endpoint uses \(f_{0.025,n_1-1,n_2-1}\), the upper uses \(f_{0.025,n_2-1,n_1-1}\). That is exactly the slide-498 two-tailed F-test convention rewritten as a CI. As always for CI↔test duality: a CI for \(\sigma_1^2/\sigma_2^2\) decides hypotheses about the variance ratio; a CI for \(\sigma_1^2-\sigma_2^2\) (which we did not construct) would decide a different hypothesis.

Medium P-value, lower tail of F
Original.
A one-sided F-test of \(H_0:\sigma_1\ge\sigma_2\) vs.\ \(H_1:\sigma_1<\sigma_2\) is conducted with \(n_1 = 8\), \(n_2 = 15\), and the observed value \(F = S_1^2/S_2^2 = 0.30\). Compute the (lower-tail) P-value using only an upper-tail F-table.
Reveal solution

Lower-tail P \(= P[F_{7,14} \le 0.30]\). Apply slide 496: \[ P[F_{7,14} \le 0.30] = P[F_{14,7} \ge 1/0.30] = P[F_{14,7} \ge 3.333]. \] From the upper-tail table, \(f_{0.05,14,7}\approx 3.529\) and \(f_{0.025,14,7}\approx 4.43\). The observed \(3.333\) lies just below the 5% point, so the P-value is just above \(0.05\), in the band \(0.05\)–\(0.06\).

At \(\alpha=0.05\), do not reject (barely). The data are marginally consistent with \(\sigma_1<\sigma_2\) but not at the conventional 5% level.

Grading rubric — minimum points to mention
  1. Recognize lower tail — need to flip via the reciprocal identity — 2 pts
  2. Swap d.f.\ correctly: \(F_{7,14}\) becomes \(F_{14,7}\) — 1 pt
  3. Compute reciprocal \(1/0.30 = 3.33\) — 1 pt
  4. Bracket the P-value between two table values \(\Rightarrow\) just above \(0.05\) — 2 pts
  5. Decision: do not reject (barely) — 1 pt
Total: 7 pts
Trail of thought — how to attack this question

The F-distribution table is almost always one-tailed; if you need the lower tail, you must flip both the value and the d.f.\ The fastest way to avoid a sign-of-tail confusion is to write down what the alternative says before reaching for the table: \(H_1:\sigma_1<\sigma_2\) means small \(F = S_1^2/S_2^2\) is evidence against \(H_0\), so the relevant area is the lower tail of \(F_{7,14}\). After the flip you are doing a perfectly normal upper-tail lookup — just on the other distribution.

Ch 22 — Q8
Hard Diagnose the error
Original.
A student tests \(H_0:\sigma_1\le\sigma_2\) vs.\ \(H_1:\sigma_1>\sigma_2\) at \(\alpha=0.05\) given \(n_1 = 9\), \(s_1^2 = 12.0\), \(n_2 = 13\), \(s_2^2 = 4.5\). Their solution reads:
F = s2²/s1² = 4.5/12.0 = 0.375. Critical value f0.05,12,8 = 3.28. 0.375 < 3.28, so do not reject H0.
Identify all errors and give the correct test, decision and P-value.
Reveal solution

Errors.

  • Wrong ratio. For \(H_1:\sigma_1>\sigma_2\), the relevant statistic is \(F = S_1^2/S_2^2\) (large values are evidence against \(H_0\)), not \(S_2^2/S_1^2\). The student's ratio is the test statistic for the opposite alternative.
  • Wrong d.f.\ in critical value. For \(F = S_1^2/S_2^2\) the d.f.\ are \((n_1-1,n_2-1) = (8,12)\), not \((12,8)\).
  • The arithmetic comparison happens to give "do not reject" only because the wrongly-computed statistic was below the critical value — the conclusion is reached by an unrelated chain of errors.

Correct solution. \(F = S_1^2/S_2^2 = 12.0/4.5 = 2.667 \sim F_{8,12}\) under the boundary of \(H_0\). Critical value \(f_{0.05,8,12} \approx 2.85\). Since \(2.667 < 2.85\), do not reject \(H_0\). The (one-sided) P-value is \(P[F_{8,12}\ge 2.667] \approx 0.062\). Conclusion (do not reject, marginally) coincidentally matches the student's verdict, but the reasoning is correct only by accident.

Grading rubric — minimum points to mention
  1. Identify wrong ratio: should be \(S_1^2/S_2^2\) — 2 pts
  2. Identify wrong d.f.\ ordering — 1 pt
  3. Compute correct \(F = 2.67\) and quote \(f_{0.05,8,12}=2.85\) — 2 pts
  4. Correct decision (do not reject) and P-value \(\approx 0.06\) — 1 pt
  5. Note that the verdict happens to agree but the reasoning is unsound — 1 pt
Total: 7 pts
Trail of thought — how to attack this question

The diagnostic skill is to read the alternative carefully and pick the ratio whose large values are evidence against \(H_0\). For \(H_1:\sigma_1>\sigma_2\), large \(F = S_1^2/S_2^2\) is the right tail to inspect; the d.f.\ then follow from the numerator and denominator order. As in earlier "diagnose-the-error" problems, a verdict reached by wrong reasoning earns no credit, even when it coincides with the correct verdict — document each step you found wrong.

Ch 22 — Q10
Hard Which test should I use?
Original. Tests slide-499's normality requirement.
An engineer wants to compare the variability of two production lines. Each sample has \(n = 50\) observations. A normality check (e.g.\ histogram, normal-quantile plot) reveals that both samples are visibly heavy-tailed and asymmetric. Should the engineer use the F-test? If not, what would you recommend?
Reveal solution

Do not use the F-test. Slide 499 states that "for the F-test to be applicable, it is essential that the populations are normally distributed." Unlike the two-sample T-test, the F-test for variances does not become CLT-robust at moderate \(n\). Heavy-tailed data inflate the apparent sample variance and produce many false rejections.

Recommended alternative. Use a non-parametric test for dispersion such as the Levene test, Brown–Forsythe test (a robust modification of Levene), or Bartlett's test only after a normalising transformation. (These tests are not in the chapter-22 syllabus, so on an exam answer it would be enough to (a) reject the F-test on grounds of non-normality, (b) name "non-parametric / robust dispersion test" as the alternative class, and (c) flag that pre-transforming the data to approximate normality is an option.)

If the goal is downstream (i.e.\ deciding pooled vs.\ Welch T-test for the means), the slide-527 recommendation applies: skip the variance comparison and use Welch's T-test directly.

Grading rubric — minimum points to mention
  1. Reject the F-test on the basis of non-normality (slide 499) — 2 pts
  2. State that CLT does not save the F-test (unlike the T-test) — 2 pts
  3. Name a robust / non-parametric alternative (Levene, Brown–Forsythe, etc.) — 1 pt
  4. Mention transformation to normality as an option — 1 pt
  5. Connect to slide 527: skip pre-test entirely if downstream is means — 1 pt
Total: 7 pts
Trail of thought — how to attack this question

The intuition for why F is fragile under non-normality: the F-statistic is the ratio of two estimates of variance, and variance is driven heavily by the tails. Heavy-tailed samples produce sample variances that are themselves highly variable, so the F-statistic departs sharply from its theoretical \(F_{\nu_1,\nu_2}\) distribution. The T-test, in contrast, depends on the sample mean, which is well-behaved by the CLT even for heavy-tailed parents at moderate \(n\). This asymmetry — T tolerates non-normality, F does not — is one of the key takeaways of chapters 19 and 22.

Ch 22 — Q11
Hard CI ↔ F-test duality, one-sided
Original.
Construct a one-sided 95% upper confidence bound for \(\sigma_1^2/\sigma_2^2\) given \(n_1 = 12\), \(s_1^2 = 1.50\), \(n_2 = 16\), \(s_2^2 = 0.80\). Use it to test \(H_0:\sigma_1\le\sigma_2\) vs.\ \(H_1:\sigma_1>\sigma_2\) at the 5% level.
Reveal solution

From \(F = (S_1^2/\sigma_1^2)/(S_2^2/\sigma_2^2)\sim F_{11,15}\), we have \(P[F_{11,15} \ge f_{0.95,11,15}] = 0.95\), and the corresponding 95% one-sided lower confidence bound for \(\sigma_1^2/\sigma_2^2\) is \[ \frac{\sigma_1^2}{\sigma_2^2} \ge \frac{S_1^2/S_2^2}{f_{0.05,\,11,\,15}}. \] For the upper-bound problem here we need \(P[F_{11,15} \le f_{0.05,11,15}] = 0.95\), giving the upper bound \[ \frac{\sigma_1^2}{\sigma_2^2} \le \frac{S_1^2}{S_2^2}\cdot f_{0.05,\,15,\,11}. \] With \(S_1^2/S_2^2 = 1.50/0.80 = 1.875\) and \(f_{0.05,\,15,\,11}\approx 2.72\): \[ \frac{\sigma_1^2}{\sigma_2^2} \le 1.875\cdot 2.72 \approx 5.10. \]

Test by duality. The one-sided test \(H_0:\sigma_1\le\sigma_2\) at \(\alpha=0.05\) rejects iff \(1\) falls outside the 95% upper bound, i.e.\ iff the lower bound for \(\sigma_1^2/\sigma_2^2\) exceeds 1. Switching to the lower-bound formula: \(\sigma_1^2/\sigma_2^2 \ge (S_1^2/S_2^2)/f_{0.05,11,15} = 1.875/2.51 \approx 0.747\). Since \(0.747 < 1\), the lower bound does not exclude 1 and the test does not reject. (Direct test: \(F=1.875\) vs.\ \(f_{0.05,11,15}\approx 2.51\) — same conclusion.)

Grading rubric — minimum points to mention
  1. Set up the pivot \((S_1^2/\sigma_1^2)/(S_2^2/\sigma_2^2)\sim F_{n_1-1,n_2-1}\) — 2 pts
  2. Derive the one-sided upper bound formula \(\sigma_1^2/\sigma_2^2 \le (S_1^2/S_2^2)\cdot f_{\alpha,\,n_2-1,\,n_1-1}\) — 2 pts
  3. Compute upper bound \(\approx 5.10\) — 1 pt
  4. Apply duality: one-sided test rejects iff lower bound \(> 1\) — 1 pt
  5. Verify with the direct F-test — 1 pt
Total: 7 pts
Trail of thought — how to attack this question

One-sided CIs and one-sided tests have to match in direction: a one-sided upper bound on \(\sigma_1^2/\sigma_2^2\) is the dual of a one-sided test that rejects when the ratio is too small. To test \(H_1:\sigma_1>\sigma_2\) (ratio too large), we want the dual one-sided lower bound. Mismatching direction is a classic error. The clearest way to keep this straight: write down the test's rejection direction first, then construct the CI on the same side.

Easy Two-sample Z, known variances
Original. Direct application of slide 506.

Easy Pooled (Student) T-test
Original; structure echoes slide-516 example.
Two normal populations are believed to share a common (unknown) variance. Samples give \(n_1 = 10\), \(\bar x_1 = 85.2\), \(s_1 = 4.5\) and \(n_2 = 12\), \(\bar x_2 = 82.1\), \(s_2 = 5.0\). At \(\alpha = 0.05\), test \(H_0:\mu_1=\mu_2\) vs.\ \(H_1:\mu_1\neq\mu_2\) using a Student pooled T-test.

Ch 23 — Q7
Medium Pooled vs Welch decision
Original. Uses slides 521–522 and 527.
A student is comparing two means and asks: "Should I run an F-test on the same data first, then pick pooled-T or Welch-T depending on whether equal variances is rejected?" Discuss why this is not recommended, and state the chapter's preferred default.
Reveal solution

Why not pre-test on the same data. Slides 521–522 explicitly warn: performing a pre-test (here an F-test for equal variances) and then choosing the means test conditional on that pre-test's outcome invalidates the P-value of the means test. The pre-test and the subsequent means test share the same data, so their outcomes are statistically dependent; the published P-value of the means test no longer corresponds to its nominal Type I error rate.

Acceptable alternatives.

  • Test for variance equality with a separate dataset, then run the means test on fresh data.
  • Justify the equal-variance assumption from prior knowledge of the process (not from the same sample).
  • Best practice (slide 527). Skip the variance pre-test entirely and use Welch's T-test by default. Welch's test loses very little power when variances are actually equal, but is much more reliable when they are not. The course's recommendation matches the modern statistics literature: "always use Welch's test."

Grading rubric — minimum points to mention
  1. Identify pre-testing on the same data as the source of the problem — 2 pts
  2. State that the conditional procedure inflates the nominal Type I error of the means test — 1 pt
  3. Note that pre-testing on a separate dataset is acceptable — 1 pt
  4. Recommend Welch as the default per slide 527 — 2 pts
  5. Justify why Welch is safe (small power loss when variances equal, robust when unequal) — 1 pt
Total: 7 pts
Trail of thought — how to attack this question

This is a methodology question, not a calculation. Two ideas to keep separate: (a) it is fine to look at sample variances to gain intuition; (b) it is not fine to use a formal hypothesis test on the same data to gate a downstream test. The reason is the same one that makes "p-hacking" bad: any decision rule that depends on a stochastic test result on the same data destroys the controlled error rate of the next step. Slide 527 is unusually direct in its recommendation; cite it explicitly on the exam.

Ch 24 — Q5
Medium Paired vs Pooled T, ρ matters
Original. Direct application of slide 555.
Two normal populations are sampled in pairs of size \(n = 12\). The within-pair correlation is estimated to be \(\rho_{XY} \approx 0.7\). Both populations have variance \(\sigma^2 = 4\).
  1. Compare the standard error of the paired test statistic to that of the pooled test statistic.
  2. Which test is more powerful here, and why?
  3. What if instead \(\rho_{XY} \approx 0\)?
Reveal solution

Per slide 552: \(\sigma_D^2 = (2\sigma^2/n)(1-\rho_{XY})\). Pooled denominator estimates \(2\sigma^2/n\); paired denominator estimates \(\sigma_D^2/n\) for the difference, but cleaner to compare \(\sigma_D^2\) vs.\ \(2\sigma^2\) (per pair).

(i) With \(\rho = 0.7\): \[ \sigma_D^2 = (2\cdot 4)(1-0.7) = 8\cdot 0.3 = 2.4, \] vs.\ pooled estimand \(2\sigma^2 = 8\). The paired SE is roughly \(\sqrt{2.4/8} = \sqrt{0.3} \approx 0.548\) of the pooled SE.

(ii) Smaller SE in the numerator’s neighbourhood ⇒ larger T-statistic ⇒ more powerful test. The paired test is much more powerful here, easily compensating for its loss of d.f.\ (\(n-1=11\) vs.\ \(2n-2=22\)).

(iii) With \(\rho_{XY} \approx 0\): \(\sigma_D^2 \approx 2\sigma^2\), so paired and pooled have similar SE — but the pooled test has more d.f.\ (smaller critical value), so the pooled test would be slightly more powerful. Pairing in the absence of positive correlation makes the test less powerful (slide 555). The lesson: pair only when there is a real reason to expect within-pair correlation.

Grading rubric — minimum points to mention
  1. Quote slide-552 identity \(\sigma_D^2 = (2\sigma^2/n)(1-\rho_{XY})\) — 2 pts
  2. Compute the SE ratio at \(\rho = 0.7\) — 1 pt
  3. Conclude paired is more powerful, despite fewer d.f. — 1 pt
  4. For \(\rho = 0\), conclude pooled wins because of d.f.\ advantage — 2 pts
  5. State the slide-555 takeaway: pair only with positive correlation — 1 pt
Total: 7 pts
Trail of thought — how to attack this question

The chapter's key conceptual identity: variance of \(D = X-Y\) is reduced by exactly the within-pair correlation. For positively correlated data — same-subject before/after measurements, twin studies, matched controls — pairing buys you a smaller SE that more than offsets the halved d.f.\ For independently chosen subjects with no shared structure, pairing is a self-inflicted loss of power. The right design question to ask before data collection: "is there a natural matching that induces positive within-pair correlation?"

Original.
A student is given a dataset with twelve subjects, each measured twice (before and after intervention), and reports:

The difference is entirely due to recognising the within-subject correlation: a correctly paired analysis exploits the \((1-\rho)\) variance reduction, while the pooled analysis squanders it.

Ch 24 — Q11
Hard Wilcoxon rank-sum, ties caveat
Adapted from slide 540 discussion.
In a Wilcoxon rank-sum test with \(m=14\), \(n=30\), the dataset contains many ties (using the slide-540 example as an analogue). When the analyst applied the standard tie-correction to the variance, the corrected variance came out negative.
  1. Why can this happen?
  2. What should the analyst do?
Reveal solution

(i) The standard tie-correction subtracts a positive quantity from \(mn(m+n+1)/12\). When the data are very heavily tied (relative to the sample size), the subtraction can exceed the uncorrected variance and produce a negative number — obviously not a legitimate variance. Slide 540 explicitly observes this in the Vv557 midterm-scores example: the data were so coarse-grained that the formal correction broke down.

  • Use an exact permutation test. Treat the actual permutation distribution of \(W_m\) under \(H_0\) (computer-enumerated) instead of relying on the normal approximation. This is the principled fix.
  • Skip the tie correction and use the uncorrected normal approximation, with a clear caveat that ties are inflating the actual variance and the reported P-value is an overestimate of the truth (so genuine rejections are still trustworthy).
  • Switch to a different test entirely — e.g.\ a chi-square test on a coarsened version of the variable — if the discreteness of the data is severe enough that rank-based methods are barely meaningful.
Slide 540 frames this as an open research area: there is no single "best" tie correction, and current statistical software offers several different conventions.

Sanity check: 0.42 sits roughly in the middle of \([0.072,\,0.677]\) on the Artanh scale, but visibly off-centre on the original scale.
